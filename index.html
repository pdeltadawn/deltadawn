Textile · American

Figural Damask Fragment

American · Mid-20th century (likely 1940s–1950s)

A small fragment of figural damask depicting a lively bar and billiard hall scene. Figures gather around pool tables, hold drinks, and socialize — a snapshot of mid-century American leisure culture woven into cloth.

This type of "conversational" or novelty damask was likely produced for upholstery in home recreation rooms, basement bars, or commercial lounges during the postwar era. The monochromatic palette emphasizes the tonal interplay between matte and sheen surfaces characteristic of damask weaving.

Textile · Found Object

Sweetgrass Basket with Lid

Unknown maker · Found object

A coiled sweetgrass basket with fitted lid, discovered at a thrift store. The coiling technique creates concentric rings visible on the lid and body, with subtle color variation in the natural grass material. The form suggests it was made for storage or as a decorative object.

Sweetgrass basketry has deep roots in African American craft traditions, particularly in the Lowcountry region of South Carolina. Without provenance, we cannot attribute this basket to a specific tradition, but its construction demonstrates skilled handwork.

Guna People · San Blas Islands, Panama

Mola Panel, Fishing Scene

1969 · Acquired by Eileen Murphy · University of Kansas Textiles + Fiber Collection

In Their Words

This section will feature writing by Guna scholars, artists, and cultural organizations — their words on mola-making, the 1925 Tule Revolution, women's labor, and cultural survival. Research in progress.

The Object

This mola was made in 1969 by a Guna woman whose name was not recorded. It depicts a fishing scene — a sailing boat, fish on lines, a turtle, an iguana — imagery from daily life in the San Blas Islands. It was made through reverse appliqué: layers of cotton stacked and cut away to reveal color beneath, secured with stitches so small they nearly disappear.

She made it knowing it would travel. Molas entered exchange with outsiders on terms Guna women chose — a practice simultaneously economic and cultural, a way of making the tradition visible on their own terms. This object was positioned toward encounter before it reached us.

The Translation Chain

In 1969 — the same year this mola was made — Eileen Murphy, then a weaving professor at the University of Kansas, purchased it. The circumstances of that transaction are not recorded. Murphy has since died, and her knowledge of the acquisition — where, from whom, what was said — went with her.

The mola entered the KU Textiles and Fiber collection. It moved from exchange into institution. What that transition meant for the object is not documented.

Decades later, it was photographed for a digital catalog. Then it was placed on a table and photographed from dozens of angles. Those photographs were processed into a point cloud, then a mesh, then a texture map, then a GLB file small enough to load in a browser.

The surface. The distribution of color. The approximate topography of layers built up through years of stitching.

Weight. The sound the fabric makes. The smell of dye. The resistance of cloth under a needle. The memory held in the maker's hands. The conversation between Murphy and the woman who made it. The name of the woman who made it.

You are looking at evidence of the encounter between a scanner and this surface. That is not nothing. It is also not the mola.
