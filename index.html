Textile · American

Figural Damask Fragment

American · Mid-20th century (likely 1940s–1950s)

A small fragment of figural damask depicting a lively bar and billiard hall scene. Figures gather around pool tables, hold drinks, and socialize — a snapshot of mid-century American leisure culture woven into cloth.

This type of "conversational" or novelty damask was likely produced for upholstery in home recreation rooms, basement bars, or commercial lounges during the postwar era. The monochromatic palette emphasizes the tonal interplay between matte and sheen surfaces characteristic of damask weaving.

Textile · Found Object

Sweetgrass Basket with Lid

Unknown maker · Found object

A coiled sweetgrass basket with fitted lid, discovered at a thrift store. The coiling technique creates concentric rings visible on the lid and body, with subtle color variation in the natural grass material. The form suggests it was made for storage or as a decorative object.

Sweetgrass basketry has deep roots in African American craft traditions, particularly in the Lowcountry region of South Carolina. Without provenance, we cannot attribute this basket to a specific tradition, but its construction demonstrates skilled handwork.

Guna People · San Blas Islands, Panama

Mola Panel, Fishing Scene

1969 · Acquired by Eileen Murphy · University of Kansas Textiles + Fiber Collection

In Their Words

This section will feature writing by Guna scholars, artists, and cultural organizations — their words on mola-making, the 1925 Tule Revolution, women's labor, and cultural survival. Research in progress.

The Object

This mola was made in 1969 by a Guna woman whose name was not recorded. It depicts a fishing scene — a sailing boat, fish on lines, a turtle, an iguana — imagery from daily life in the San Blas Islands. It was made through reverse appliqué: layers of cotton stacked and cut away to reveal color beneath, secured with stitches so small they nearly disappear.

She made it knowing it would travel. Molas entered exchange with outsiders on terms Guna women chose — a practice simultaneously economic and cultural, a way of making the tradition visible on their own terms. This object was positioned toward encounter before it reached us.

The Translation Chain

In 1969 — the same year this mola was made — Eileen Murphy, then a weaving professor at the University of Kansas, purchased it. The circumstances of that transaction are not recorded. Murphy has since died, and her knowledge of the acquisition — where, from whom, what was said — went with her.

The mola entered the KU Textiles and Fiber collection. It moved from exchange into institution. What that transition meant for the object is not documented.

Decades later, it was photographed for a digital catalog. Then it was placed on a table and photographed from dozens of angles. Those photographs were processed into a point cloud, then a mesh, then a texture map, then a GLB file small enough to load in a browser.

The surface. The distribution of color. The approximate topography of layers built up through years of stitching.

Weight. The sound the fabric makes. The smell of dye. The resistance of cloth under a needle. The memory held in the maker's hands. The conversation between Murphy and the woman who made it. The name of the woman who made it.

You are looking at evidence of the encounter between a scanner and this surface. That is not nothing. It is also not the mola.

Uzbekistan · Central Asia

Uzbek Ikat Panel

[Date to be confirmed] · Acquired by Mary Anne Jordan · University of Kansas Textiles + Fiber Collection

In Their Words

This section will feature writing by Uzbek textile scholars, contemporary ikat weavers, and Central Asian cultural historians — their words on the abr tradition, the labor of resist-dyeing, and the survival of this practice across Soviet collectivization and into the present. Research in progress.

The Object

This panel is an example of abr — the Uzbek term for ikat, meaning "cloud." The name describes what the technique produces: patterns with soft, feathered edges that seem to shift and blur as the cloth moves. Those blurred lines are not imprecision. They are the record of a calculation made before weaving began, when bundles of warp threads were tied, dipped in dye, re-tied, and dipped again — each step building toward a pattern visible only in the mind of the dyer until the loom finally revealed it.

Uzbek ikat production was centered in the Fergana Valley — Margilan, Namangan, Kokand — and in the older trade cities of Bukhara and Samarkand. These textiles moved along the Silk Road as luxury goods, traded across Central Asia, Persia, and into South Asia and Europe. The work of making them was divided: men typically wove, while the dyeing knowledge — the calculations of resist, sequence, and color — was often held by specialist dyers whose expertise was a form of intellectual and economic power.

How this panel came to Professor Mary Anne Jordan, and through her to the KU collection, is not yet fully documented here. That chain of hands — the dyer, the weaver, the traders, the circumstances of its departure from Uzbekistan — remains to be traced.

The Translation Chain

Mary Anne Jordan, a professor in the KU Textiles and Fiber program, acquired this panel at a time and in circumstances not yet recorded in this archive. [To be expanded as research continues.]

Uzbek ikats entered Western collections through several channels from the mid-19th century onward: diplomatic gifts, colonial-era acquisition during Russian imperial expansion into Central Asia, the mid-20th century tourist trade, and later through dealers specializing in Central Asian textiles. Which of these paths this panel traveled is an open question.

Soviet collectivization in the 1920s–30s reorganized ikat production into state factories, severing some lineages of specialist knowledge. The panel's date of manufacture — and therefore the conditions under which it was made — matters to the story of what it is.

The surface patterning. The color distribution across the panel. The texture of the weave structure and the slight irregularities where the resist-dyed threads met the loom.

The hand of the cloth — its weight, drape, the way silk moves differently from cotton. The smell of natural dyes. The sheen that changes with light and angle in ways no static render captures. The knowledge carried in the hands of the dyer who calculated the pattern before the first thread was bound.

The scan preserves a surface. The ikat was made to be worn, to move, to be seen in changing light. What you see here is one fixed encounter with it.

Uzbekistan · Fergana Valley

Cut Velvet Panel

Rasuljon Mirzaakhmedon · Acquired by Mary Anne Jordan · University of Kansas Textiles + Fiber Collection

In Their Words

Rasuljon Mirzaakhmedon is a living master weaver from Margilan in the Fergana Valley — one of the few practitioners keeping the traditional silk velvet technique alive. This section will feature his words on the practice of cut velvet weaving, the knowledge it carries, and what it means to make these objects now. Research and outreach in progress.

The Object

This panel was woven by Rasuljon Mirzaakhmedon in Margilan, Uzbekistan — the center of silk velvet production in the Fergana Valley for centuries. The technique is called bakhmal: a pile weave in which loops of silk thread are raised on a loom and then cut, producing a surface of dense, light-catching fiber. The cutting is what makes it velvet. The height of the pile, the precision of the cut, the quality of the silk — these are decisions the weaver makes with each pass of the blade.

Unlike the ikat in this exhibition, whose maker's name was never recorded, this object arrives with its maker present. Mirzaakhmedon has been recognized internationally as a master of traditional Uzbek textile arts. That recognition is itself a form of translation — the work is valued, circulated, documented. What that means for the work, and for the weaver, is a question this archive holds without answering.

The Translation Chain

Mary Anne Jordan acquired this panel — the circumstances of that acquisition are not yet documented here. [To be expanded as research continues.]

That Mirzaakhmedon's name is attached to this object — that the maker is known — marks a different kind of entry into this collection than the anonymous hands behind the ikat or the mola. The knowledge of who made it does not resolve the questions of how it traveled, or what its presence in a Kansas university collection means. It reframes them.
